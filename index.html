What it is

You use tech and in order to use that tech, you have to have data. You sign up for a bank account and give your sensitive data and then you can't remember the same password a billion times so you use it once. Well what happens when one guy gets your data. They can do an incredible amount of damage. They can look at stuff like:

  • Payment Information
  • Passwords
  • Social Security Numbers
  • Medical Information
  • Federal Documents
  • Signatures
  • Content dependent on the service

This is horrendous, but you can obviously try to change your passwords and protect yourself when these events occur, but most businesses don't even tell you immediately. Some wait months and even in those months don't accomodate or fix the bug. Obviously, having your data given to someone you didn't authorize to is horrible but businesses can use the information that you give them already and hand them to another person you didn't authorize at all, but 'legally.' Essentially, instead of someone else hacking into the business and stealing it, the business just gives it to someone else entirely and makes a quick buck as well. They do this under the fact that you signed the terms of conditions, and they can do anything now. Everyone has a right to privacy and have it protected but these businesses aren't held accountable for these cybersecurity failures with most not even offering accomodation to those who fell prey to the breach. It is time that these businesses are held accountable and can finally start to work so something of the manner can't occur again.

Businesses

As we move toward the age of technology, we are relying on services and companies to store our personal information on the cloud. But what if that information gets breached? What if everyoneâ€™s personal information was accessible to anyone, including people with malicious intent?

In September of 2017, Equifax announced that the information of over 143 million consumers were exposed in a data breach. The issue here? The breach happened several weeks ago, and no announcement was made until after the initial chaos. The problem here is that Equifax was not completely transparent to their consumers, hiding the breach in an attempt to mitigate the damage.

But also in the year 2017, Facebook had a scandal, the Cambridge Analytica scandal. People were furious over this breach of privacy. How could this hacker group 'Cambridge Analytica' do this? They didn't, because Cambridge Analytica isn't a hacker group, it's a business that buys your data from companies like Facebook and does all sorts of data crunching on them. The problem was how they did this without comsumer's consent and that was a big problem. The company breached data and still made a pretty penny, which most people were furious over. This shows that data privacy isn't just business vs hacker but business vs people and it's time for the people to have the power to win.

It is time for the businesses to be held accountable, to have to hold this data in greater security with more government regulations over security and a greater emphasis on cybersecurity. The businesses need to be fined for data breaches and for breaching the privacy of their consumers. They need to have a more transparent terms of conditions, one that can protect them from liabilities but cannot allow them to abuse their power. There needs to be a control of the power they hold in using that data for ads that can influence government elections like in the 2016 election.
